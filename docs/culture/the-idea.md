# The idea — what this is all for

**In one line: this is a governed gateway for autonomous AI work — a
controlled layer between what a person wants and the whole sprawl of their
digital life, where AI can carry out entire initiatives on their behalf
without losing track of where facts came from, overstepping what it is
allowed to do, or dissolving into chat that no one can replay.**

## The problem this exists to solve

AI can now produce work — code, documents, decisions — faster than any
person can check it. That speed creates two problems at once:

1. **You can't take its word for it.** A model will state something wrong
   with total confidence, and it is a poor judge of its own work. So the
   more it produces, the more unchecked claims pile up.
2. **It forgets.** Each session starts from nothing. What it "knew" lived
   in a chat transcript, and transcripts rot — they get summarized, lost,
   or contradicted, and no one can replay exactly how a conclusion was
   reached.

So if you want AI to do *real, accumulating* work — work that builds on
itself and that you can trust later — you need something underneath it that
makes the work **durable** (it survives), **checked** (a second, independent
set of eyes saw it), and **traceable** (you can see where every piece came
from) — and that does all this *without a human babysitting every step.*

## The larger problem: AI has no lawful body to act through

That is the surface problem. Underneath it is a bigger one.

A person's work is spread across a **digital body** — email, calendar,
files, repos, chats, docs, tasks, products, accounts, dashboards,
preferences, obligations, and their public and private identity. And a
person's agency is spread across a **digital persona** — their voice,
priorities, boundaries, relationships, commitments, taste, strategy,
reputation, and authority.

Today's AI can answer, draft, summarize, search, call a tool, or run a
fixed workflow — but always trapped *inside a single session*. It never
naturally becomes a trusted operator across a person's whole digital life,
because **it has no lawful body to operate through**: nothing governs what
it may touch, what it may change, when it may act *as* you, and what it must
get checked before any of it becomes real.

This project's deeper purpose is to give AI that lawful body.

## What this lets a person do

It becomes a **gateway** — a single governed point that sits between:

- what you *mean*,
- what your tools and accounts actually *contain*,
- what *work* needs to happen,
- what *authority* the AI is permitted,
- what *consequences* are acceptable, and
- what must be *checked* before anything becomes real.

The shift that buys you: you stop governing **prompts** and start governing
**initiatives**. A prompt is a single request — it produces an answer and
ends. An initiative is a living campaign that runs across tools, time,
evidence, people, decisions, and consequences. Normal AI helps you *inside a
moment*; this is built to help across *the whole life of the work* — and to
do it without ever taking authority that is yours.

## Who this is for

Not only experts, founders, researchers, or teams already building with AI.
It is for anyone who wants to move through the digital world with more
safety,
clarity, and agency.

A person's digital life is already too large to hold in your head at once —
messages, files, accounts, subscriptions, purchases, forms, tasks, jobs,
studies, hobbies, communities, a public identity and a private memory — and
with it the constant risk of being misled, overwhelmed, or quietly acted
upon by systems you don't fully understand. The gateway is meant to be a
clean, single contact surface with that reality.

- **For a casual user** — understand a confusing email, avoid a scam,
  organize a project, prepare for a call, compare options, study a topic, or
  make a safer decision *before* clicking, signing, buying, replying, or
  sharing.
- **For a worker** — turn scattered obligations into tracked work, keep
  context across meetings and documents, prepare decisions, and protect your
  voice and your commitments.
- **For a builder or researcher** — scale up into platform-level
  initiatives: product arcs, code work, knowledge maps, review systems,
  governed agents, and long-running autonomous projects.

The point is the same at every level: **raise the floor and remove the
ceiling.** Raise the floor, because anyone who can hold a conversation
should be able to use AI to deal with the digital world more safely and
effectively. Remove the ceiling, because the *same* system scales up into
serious, AI-first work — initiatives, research, companies, platforms, and
governed autonomy.

Ontum is not meant to turn everyone into an operator. It is meant to let
**ordinary conversation become an operating skill.**

## The first use cases

These are the kinds of work the gateway is built to govern, from an everyday
question to a platform-scale initiative. The *engine* described further down
is what is real and under construction; these are what it is **for** —
concrete on purpose.

**1. A single place to run your own initiatives.** Loose intent scattered
across ideas, docs, repos, tasks, and conversations becomes durable, tracked
work.
> *"Explore whether this product direction is worth pursuing."*

It gathers the context, names the unknowns, lays out options, assigns
reviewers, logs its own assumptions, and brings only the decisions that
matter back to you. *A chatbot answers once; this keeps the initiative
alive.*

**2. Research that doesn't rot.** Findings usually die in tabs and
summaries where you can't tell what was checked, what's stale, or which
conclusion leaned on which source. Here each claim is a tracked unit with
its source, its review, and its history.
> *"Track the best arguments for and against consequence-based AI governance."*

You get a living map of claims, objections, sources, open questions, and
your own judgments. *Retrieval (RAG) fetches relevant text; this governs the
whole life of a piece of knowledge.*

**3. An operating system for product and code work.** Coding is the easy
part; product work also needs scope, acceptance, review, history, and safe
sequencing.
> *"Turn this architecture conversation into a staged plan with tasks, tests, and review."*

It creates the work, checks it, routes it, keeps the decision history, and
never lets a worker approve its own output. *An autonomous coding agent acts
and grades itself; this wraps governance around the agent instead of
pretending the agent is the whole system.*

**4. A digital chief of staff.** Email, calendar, docs, messages, and tasks
don't compose into one picture on their own. The gateway watches across them
and surfaces only the choices worth your attention.
> *"Tell me what changed overnight that affects my active initiatives."*

Not an inbox summary — a consequence-aware briefing: what changed, what it
touches, what needs a decision, and what can safely proceed. *Automation
fires fixed rules; this weighs relevance against your live work.*

**5. A persona gateway — representing you, with limits.** AI can copy your
tone in one document, but it doesn't reliably know *when* it may speak as
you, *to whom*, or *with what risk*. Here your persona is governed: voice,
commitments, boundaries, and the thresholds above which it must stop and
ask.
> *"Reply in my voice, but never commit me to dates, money, partnerships, or public statements without my sign-off."*

*Custom instructions shape wording; this governs authority.*

**6. Acting inside a consequence envelope.** You can't list every allowed
action in a complex life, but you can bound the *results* you will accept.
> *"You may reorganize my notes as long as nothing is deleted, every original path is preserved, and every move can be undone."*

That is stronger than *"you may move files"* — it defines what the world is
allowed to look like *afterward*. This is the bridge from tool-using AI to
AI you can trust with bounded independence.

## Two things, and don't confuse them

To see how the engine delivers all that, hold one distinction firmly: there
is a **goal** and there is an **engine for reaching it**. Mixing them up is
the easiest mistake to make here.

**The goal is called Ontum.** Picture a living body of knowledge where
every piece of meaning carries its own papers — where it came from, what it
is allowed to mean, who checked it, and how it has changed over time. A
fact in Ontum is not a loose sentence; it is a unit that knows its own
boundary and history and can refuse to be used in a way that breaks it.

It helps to say what Ontum is *not*:

- It is **not a database.** Rows don't know their own history, and they
  never refuse a bad write.
- It is **not a wiki.** Wiki pages aren't independently reviewed, and they
  don't carry proof of where their claims came from.
- It is **not a chat history.** Chat forgets, and you can't replay it.

Ontum is meant to be a self-describing, self-checking fabric of meaning.
That is the building.

**The engine is this repository.** This repo is *not* Ontum — it is the
workshop and the discipline that could keep such a fabric alive, and the
gateway the use cases above are built on. (An earlier AI session once called
the engine "what the goal is trying to be." That was a mistake, caught and
corrected;
this whole document exists to keep it corrected.)

## How the engine works, in plain rules

Four rules carry almost everything — and together they are what makes the
gateway *governed* instead of just another agent let loose on your accounts:

1. **The files are the workplace; the workers are temporary.** The work
   lives in files on disk. An AI "worker" — one run of the AI, also called a
   *session* — shows up, does one bounded job, and leaves; the files stay.
   Unlike an app that holds everything in memory and loses it all when it
   crashes, you can stop a worker dead in the middle of a step and lose
   nothing: the next worker reads the files and continues.
2. **Nothing is trusted just because it says so.** A worker judges its own
   work and stands behind it — that conviction is its case — but its own
   approval is **never the one that counts.** A second, *independent* reader
   casts the deciding check, and one human has the final say. (Contrast the
   usual AI agent, which grades its own homework and ships it.)
3. **Memory lives in a permanent record, not in conversation.** Decisions
   and results are written to an append-only log — you only ever add to it,
   never rewrite it. Anyone can replay the whole history from that log and
   get the exact same state. Chat is where things are discussed; the log is
   where things are *true.*
4. **One person steers by decisions, not by labor.** The owner approves
   *directions* — "yes, pursue this" — and the machine carries out all the
   small steps under that approval. He is the last word on what matters,
   but he is not the one running the commands.

None of these are promises. The system is built to *enforce* them: a worker
can't approve its own work because the system refuses it, not because it was
asked nicely; memory is trustworthy because the log is append-only, not
because we vow to behave. Whether it keeps enforcing them as it grows is
something you check, not something you take on faith.

## How this enhances existing AI patterns

Each familiar AI pattern is a piece of what's here — this composes them into
something larger:

| The familiar thing | What it becomes here |
|---|---|
| **Chatbot** — answers inside a session | **Gateway** — carries work across sessions, tools, and time |
| **Memory** — what a model recalls | **Record** — what the system can *prove*, and replay |
| **Prompt** — produces output, then ends | **Initiative** — has state, history, permissions, checks, and consequences |
| **Tool use** — "may the AI call this function?" | **Governed action** — "what *state* is the AI allowed to create?" |
| **RAG** — retrieves relevant chunks | **Provenance fabric** — meaning units that carry source, boundary, history, and review |
| **Agent** — acts, and often judges itself | **A staffed team** — the one who works, the one who independently checks, the owner who decides, and the record that proves it, kept separate (no one casts the deciding check on their own work) |
| **Automation** — repeats a fixed rule | **Autonomy** — pursues an approved initiative inside bounded authority |

## What "finished" would look like

The goal is approached in arcs — large, named pushes, each with a clear
picture of what *done* looks like for it. The engine always holds the
current, exact list (see *Where this comes from*). Grouped by what each is
really for:

- **An engine you can trust enough to point throwaway AI sessions at.**
  Every state can be rebuilt from files; every claim passed a second pair
  of eyes; the system can tell itself to slow down before it overwhelms the
  owner.
- **The owner keeps power without doing more work as the system grows.** He
  sees the important decisions with their context, judges them in minutes
  from any device, and the queue refuses to flood him.
- **The system staffs and governs itself.** It launches its own work,
  finds its own reviewers, restarts what has died, chooses which AI models
  are allowed to judge based on measurement rather than guesswork, and even
  has a lawful way to make a *bet* — a marked, reversible, expiring guess —
  before the evidence is in, without corrupting what is known to be true.
- **The system always knows where it is.** What it is working on, what
  belongs to what, who is active, what stage each piece is at — a rough
  sketch, a half-finished draft, or finished work sitting in the wrong place
  — and what is safe to do without overstepping the owner's authority.
- **The system can be seen and steered without becoming a second source of
  truth.** A surface where you can watch the work, request work, and even
  simulate a change before proposing it — without ever creating a second,
  silently-editable copy of the truth.
- **The custom notation it invents for meaning becomes machine-checked, not
  just prose.** (The project is also designing its own small, formal way of
  writing meaning down.) Every rule of that notation is verified
  automatically on every run, and there is a calibrated test for whether
  structure hidden in an encoding can actually be recovered.
- **The work polices its own quality.** Weak tests and sprawling,
  unbounded context get refused the same way fake work does — quality
  becomes something the system enforces, not an honor system.
- **Outside review becomes real work, not just notes.** A critique from
  another AI or another person comes back in as checked, queued work with a
  return path, instead of read-only commentary that rots in a thread.

These arcs are the gateway being built piece by piece; the use cases above
are what those pieces add up to. (Each grouping names several arcs; the
engine's own list is the authority, this is the human-readable map of it.)

## The measure is a slope, not a demo

Success here is **not** one impressive demonstration. It is a *trend*: over
time, more and more of the system becomes solid, checkable, replayable
code, and less of it is the AI free-handing things in the moment. The
hand-made share shrinks; the reliable floor underneath it grows. A demo
that works once and is never replayed proves nothing. *(We do not yet have
a number that measures this slope — it is a named gap, not a solved one.)*

## The deep bet, underneath all of it

This is the longest-range, least-proven part — named as a bet, not a
result. The wager: a **small set of rules you can hold in your head** can
govern an **unbounded** amount of meaning, the way a handful of grammar
rules produce endlessly many valid sentences. You work the small handle —
the finite rules — and inherit reach over everything they generate, without
ever having to hold the whole space in your head.

That is the same shape as the four rules above: a few fixed separations
governing open-ended work — now pointed at *meaning itself*. And it only
holds if every generated piece carries its own papers — where it came from,
who checked it. Without that, the unbounded space those rules produce is
just unbounded *nonsense* — which is exactly why the engine underneath is
built to be this strict about where things come from and who checked them.

## The words, plainly

The insider terms, each in one line — a bridge into the project's
vocabulary, so this document is a door to the rest of the repo, not a wall.
A few of these don't appear above at all; they're here because you'll meet
them the moment you read anything else.

- **Ontum** — the goal: the living, self-checking fabric of meaning (the
  building).
- **the loop / the substrate** — the engine in this repo (the workshop).
  Files = workplace, workers = temporary, the record = memory.
- **the gateway** — the engine seen from the user's side: the one governed
  layer between a person's intent and their digital life.
- **the log** — the append-only record that is the single source of truth.
- **a fold** — any view of the system computed fresh from the log (so the
  view can never drift from the truth).
- **an atom** — one unit of work moving through the engine.
- **a gate / a node** — a checkpoint that judges a piece of work and can
  refuse it.
- **an arc / epic** — a large named push toward the goal, with a defined
  "done."
- **the owner / bdo** — the one person with the final say, who steers by
  approving directions.
- **the viewport** — the owner's own copy of the files, the surface he
  reads from.
